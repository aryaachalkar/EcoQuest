What is this lesson about?
Chapter overview & NCERT alignment

Every day, water is used in our homes for bathing, cooking, washing and flushing — this water becomes sewage after use. Wastewater Story (NCERT Science, Class 7, Chapter 18) teaches us where this dirty water goes, how it is cleaned, and why safe disposal of wastewater is critical for health and the environment.

This sub-section introduces the concept of wastewater, its sources, what pollutants it contains, and the role of the sewage treatment plant (STP) in making dirty water safe again before releasing it into rivers or reusing it. It also stresses the link between poor sanitation and waterborne diseases like cholera, typhoid and dysentery — a real and ongoing problem in urban and rural India.

The NCERT lesson connects directly to the Sustainable Development Goal 6 — Clean Water and Sanitation — and is part of the Maharashtra SSC Standard 7 science syllabus as well.

Key Concepts at a Glance
Core ideas you will learn in this sub-section
What is Wastewater?
Water that has been used in homes, factories or agriculture and is contaminated with waste, chemicals or pathogens.
Sewage Composition
Sewage contains water, organic waste, detergents, nitrates, phosphates, pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites) and heavy metals.
Sewage Treatment Plant (STP)
An STP cleans wastewater in three stages — physical (screening), biological (aeration, sedimentation) and chemical — before it can be released safely.
Waterborne Diseases
Cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A, dysentery and polio spread through contaminated water. Proper treatment prevents outbreaks.
Sanitation & Open Defecation
Open defecation contaminates groundwater and soil. Swachh Bharat Mission aims to build household toilets and eliminate open defecation in India.
Why Untreated Sewage is Dangerous
Dumping untreated sewage in rivers depletes dissolved oxygen, kills aquatic life and renders rivers unfit for drinking or irrigation.

NCERT pg. 220–221

Wastewater (also called sewage) is any water that has been used and contaminated. It is produced from multiple sources:

  • Domestic/household — kitchen sink water, toilet flush, bathing water, washing machine water
  • Industrial — factories release chemicals, dyes, acids and heavy metals like mercury and lead into water
  • Agricultural — excess fertilisers (nitrates, phosphates) and pesticides wash off farmland into water bodies
  • Hospitals — medical waste and pharmaceutical chemicals
NCERT Key Fact: Sewage is 99.9% water. The remaining 0.1% contains dissolved and suspended impurities — but that tiny fraction is enough to make the water extremely dangerous and unusable.
SSC Link: Maharashtra Board Standard 7 includes the concept of "safe disposal of waste" — this connects directly to managing wastewater at home and in factories.
NCERT pg. 221–222

Sewage is a complex mixture. It contains both dissolved substances (which mix into water) and suspended substances (which float or sink).

  • Organic matter — faeces, food waste, vegetable and fruit scraps
  • Inorganic salts — nitrates, phosphates from fertilisers, chlorides
  • Pathogens — disease-causing bacteria (E. coli, Vibrio cholerae), viruses (Hepatitis A, Poliovirus), parasites (Entamoeba)
  • Detergents & soaps — cause foam and reduce oxygen in water bodies
  • Heavy metals — lead, mercury, arsenic from industries
  • Toxic chemicals — dyes, pesticides, pharmaceuticals
Dissolved oxygen (DO) in water is essential for aquatic life. When sewage enters a river, microbes decomposing organic waste consume all the dissolved oxygen, suffocating fish and other organisms — this is called Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD). High BOD = highly polluted water.
NCERT pg. 222–224

A Sewage Treatment Plant (STP) or Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) is a facility that processes wastewater in stages to remove impurities before the water is returned to the environment or reused.

  • Step 1 — Physical treatment (Screening & Sedimentation): Sewage flows through bar screens to remove large objects like rags and plastic. Then it flows into a grit tank where sand and small stones settle. Finally, water enters a large settling tank where suspended solids (called sludge) sink to the bottom.
  • Step 2 — Biological treatment (Aeration): The remaining water is pumped into an aeration tank, where air is bubbled through it. This encourages aerobic bacteria to rapidly break down organic matter. Bacteria consume the organic waste and convert it into harmless substances. The mixture then flows to a secondary settling tank, where bacterial mass settles as activated sludge.
  • Step 3 — Chemical treatment (Chlorination): The clarified water is disinfected using chlorine or ozone to kill any remaining pathogens. The water is now safe to discharge into rivers or to reuse for irrigation.
  • Sludge treatment: The collected sludge is dried in sludge-drying beds or treated in a biogas digester, where anaerobic bacteria produce biogas (methane) — used as fuel. The dried sludge can be used as fertiliser (manure) for agriculture.
Eco-Connection: STPs turn waste into resources — biogas (fuel), treated water (irrigation), and dried sludge (compost/manure). This is a real example of circular economy in environmental science.
NCERT pg. 224–225

Sanitation refers to the safe collection and disposal of human waste. It is a basic human right and a key part of public health. In India, lack of proper sanitation has been a major challenge.

  • Open defecation — practising in fields, open grounds, near rivers — allows faecal bacteria to enter groundwater and surface water, spreading diseases.
  • India's Swachh Bharat Mission (Clean India Mission), launched in 2014, aimed to build 100 million household toilets and make India open-defecation free (ODF) by 2019.
  • Many rural areas still lack proper sewage networks. Wastewater flows directly into rivers like the Ganga, Yamuna and local rivers in Maharashtra.
  • Sewerage system — a network of underground pipes that carries sewage from households and industries to STPs.
Think about it: 80% of India's water pollution comes from untreated sewage directly discharged into rivers. Every household toilet connected to a sewage line and treated at an STP prevents disease and saves lives.
NCERT pg. 225

Waterborne diseases spread when contaminated water is used for drinking, cooking or washing. These diseases kill millions globally each year, and disproportionately affect children under five.

  • Cholera — caused by Vibrio cholerae bacteria; symptoms: severe watery diarrhoea and dehydration
  • Typhoid — caused by Salmonella typhi; symptoms: high fever, weakness, stomach pain
  • Hepatitis A — caused by a virus; affects the liver; spreads through contaminated water and food
  • Dysentery — caused by Shigella bacteria or Entamoeba histolytica (amoeba); bloody diarrhoea
  • Polio — caused by poliovirus; can cause permanent paralysis; nearly eradicated in India through vaccination + sanitation
Prevention: Boil drinking water, use water purifiers (RO/UV), maintain personal hygiene, wash hands with soap, ensure food is cooked properly, support proper sewage disposal systems in your community.
