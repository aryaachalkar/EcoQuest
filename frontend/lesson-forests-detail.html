What Is a Forest? NCERT Class 7 Ch. 17 · SSC Maharashtra Class 7 Science

A forest is a large, densely wooded area that forms a complex, self-sustaining ecosystem. Forests are not merely collections of trees — they are intricate communities of producers, consumers, and decomposers that interact continuously with one another and with the non-living environment (soil, water, sunlight, and air).

According to your NCERT Science Class 7 textbook (Chapter 17), a forest is described as a system where plants, animals, and micro-organisms live together, each depending on the others. This interdependence is what keeps the forest healthy and self-renewing. The same concept appears in the SSC Maharashtra Board Class 7 Science chapter on "Natural Resources and their Conservation."

📖 Textbook Reference (NCERT Class 7 Science, Ch. 17): "A forest is a system in which plants, animals, and micro-organisms live together and depend on each other for their needs. It is a biotic community consisting of producers, consumers, and decomposers."

Types of Forests in India NCERT Geography Class 9, Ch. 5 · SSC Geography Class 8–9

India's forests are classified into five main types based on climate, rainfall, and altitude. This classification is covered in NCERT Geography Class 9 (Chapter 5 — Natural Vegetation and Wildlife) and SSC Maharashtra Board Geography (Classes 8–9).

🌧️ Tropical Evergreen Forests Heavy rainfall

Found in Western Ghats, northeast India (Assam, Meghalaya), and Andaman & Nicobar. Receive over 200 cm of annual rainfall. Trees never shed all leaves at once.

📌 Example species: Rosewood, Mahogany, Ebony

🍂 Tropical Deciduous Forests Most widespread

Most common forest type in India — Madhya Pradesh, Odisha, Maharashtra. Trees shed leaves in dry season to conserve water. Divided into Moist and Dry deciduous.

📌 Example species: Teak, Sal, Shisham

🌵 Tropical Thorn Forests Arid regions

Found in Rajasthan, Gujarat, and parts of Punjab. Very low rainfall (<70 cm). Thorny shrubs and small trees adapted to conserve water.

📌 Example species: Khejri (State tree of Rajasthan), Babul (Acacia)

⛰️ Montane Forests Altitude-based

Found along the Himalayas and Nilgiris. Vegetation changes with altitude: tropical → temperate → alpine meadows as you climb higher.

📌 Example species: Oak, Deodar, Rhododendron, Silver Fir

🌊 Mangrove Forests Coastal / Tidal

Unique salt-tolerant forests found in tidal zones — the Sundarbans (West Bengal) is the world's largest mangrove forest. These forests protect coastlines from cyclones and erosion, and are nurseries for fish and crabs. Maharashtra's Konkan coast also has mangrove patches.

📌 Example species: Sundari (gives Sundarbans its name), Avicennia, Rhizophora
🌿 Maharashtra Connect (SSC Board): Maharashtra has tropical deciduous forests in the Sahyadri (Western Ghats) range and mangrove patches along the Konkan coast. The Sahyadri forests are home to leopards, Indian giant squirrels, and over 1,500 plant species — many found nowhere else in the world.

Layers of a Forest Ecosystem NCERT Science Class 7, Ch. 17 — Vertical Stratification

A forest is not flat — it is organized into vertical layers or strata, each forming its own micro-habitat with distinct plants and animals. This vertical structure is a key concept in NCERT Science Class 7 (Chapter 17) and explains how one forest can support thousands of different species simultaneously.

LayerHeightKey FeaturesExample Organisms
🌳 Emergent Layer 30 – 60 m Tallest trees rising above canopy; full sun & strong winds Teak, Deodar, Rosewood; eagles, large hornbills
🍃 Canopy Layer 20 – 30 m Dense, interlocking branches; traps most sunlight; highest animal diversity Monkeys, leopards, parrots, flying squirrels
🌿 Understorey 5 – 20 m Shade-adapted smaller trees & shrubs; diffused light Bamboo, wild banana, tree ferns, slender loris
🌱 Shrub Layer 1 – 5 m Dense shrubs, saplings, young trees competing for light Wild berry bushes, coffee plants, small reptiles
🍂 Forest Floor 0 – 1 m Very little light; decaying leaf litter; nutrient recycling hub Earthworms, beetles, fungi, mushrooms, centipedes

Why Forests Are Important NCERT Science Class 7 Ch. 17 · EVS Classes 3–5

Environmental Functions

Oxygen production & CO₂ absorption: Through photosynthesis, trees absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen. A single mature tree absorbs about 22 kg of CO₂ per year. Forests act as carbon sinks — they slow climate change by storing carbon in their biomass and soil.

Water cycle regulation: Forests are critical to the water cycle. Tree roots absorb rainwater, which slowly releases into groundwater and streams. Trees release water vapour through transpiration, which forms clouds and eventually falls as rain — forests literally make their own rain.

Soil protection: Tree roots bind soil together, preventing erosion by wind and rain. In hilly areas, forests prevent dangerous landslides. When forests are cleared, the topsoil is quickly washed away, making land infertile.

Biodiversity: About 80% of the world's known land biodiversity lives in forests. Every organism — from a tiny soil microbe to a Bengal tiger — plays an interconnected role in maintaining the forest's balance.

Importance for Humans (NCERT EVS Classes 3–5)

Forests provide timber for furniture and buildings, fuelwood for cooking, and hundreds of non-timber forest products — honey, medicinal herbs (neem, turmeric, tulsi, ashwagandha), rubber, bamboo, and lac. India's tribal communities have lived with and protected forests for thousands of years, making them our best conservationists.

📚 NCERT EVS Class 5 (Ch. 5 — Seeds, Seeds, Seeds) Connect: This chapter shows how forest biodiversity depends on seed dispersal — seeds travel by wind, water, and animals, ensuring the forest naturally regenerates. Without diverse seed dispersal, a forest cannot sustain itself.

Threats to Forests NCERT Science Class 8 Ch. 7 · SSC Class 8–9 Environmental Studies

Deforestation is the largest threat — clearing forests for agriculture, urban expansion, roads, dams, and industries. India loses thousands of hectares of dense forest every year. When forests are cut, animals lose their homes, soil erodes, groundwater depletes, and local rainfall decreases.

Forest fires — both natural (lightning) and human-caused (burning for land clearing or accidental fires) — destroy vast areas. Uttarakhand, Maharashtra (Vidarbha), and Odisha face recurring forest fires every summer.

Overgrazing by cattle destroys undergrowth and prevents natural forest regeneration. Young saplings are eaten before they can grow into mature trees.

Poaching and illegal logging: Endangered animals like tigers, elephants, and rhinos are killed for body parts. Rare trees like sandalwood and teak are illegally felled for high market prices.

📊 India Data (State of Forest Report 2021): India has approximately 7,13,789 sq km of total forest and tree cover. However, 44.6% of this is classified as "open forest" — degraded, sparse trees with less than 40% canopy density. Only 3% is classified as "very dense forest."

Conservation — Protecting Forests NCERT Science Class 8 Ch. 7 · Forest Rights Act 2006

Reserved Forests, Protected Forests, and Village Forests are legal categories under the Indian Forest Act where cutting trees is restricted or prohibited. India has 106 National Parks and 567 Wildlife Sanctuaries specifically to protect forest ecosystems and endangered species.

The Forest Rights Act, 2006 — an important law covered in SSC civics — formally recognises the rights of tribal and forest-dwelling communities who have lived in and protected India's forests for generations. These communities are now legally recognised as forest guardians.

The famous Chipko Movement (1973, Uttarakhand) is a landmark example of community conservation. Villagers — especially women — literally hugged trees to stop contractors from cutting them. The word Chipko means "to hug" in Hindi. This became a worldwide symbol of peaceful environmental protest.

Social forestry and agroforestry programmes encourage planting trees along roadsides, farmlands, and degraded lands. Government campaigns like Van Mahotsav (Forest Festival) and Ek Ped Maa Ke Naam motivate millions of students and citizens to plant trees annually.

🌱 Your Role as a Student: Plant native trees (not ornamental exotic species) in your school or home. Participate in Van Mahotsav. Refuse single-use plastic — plastic litter kills forest animals. Write letters to your local municipality if you see trees being unnecessarily cut. Every action counts!

Quick Revision Notes Key points for exam preparation

Forest — Definition

A biotic community of producers (plants), consumers (animals), and decomposers (fungi, bacteria) that live and interact together in a natural ecosystem.

5 Forest Types in India

Tropical Evergreen · Tropical Deciduous · Thorn & Scrub · Montane · Mangrove (Sundarbans = world's largest).

5 Forest Layers (Strata)

Emergent → Canopy → Understorey → Shrub Layer → Forest Floor. Each layer is a distinct habitat.

Water Cycle Role

Trees absorb rain → store in groundwater → release via transpiration → forms clouds → rainfall. Forests make their own rain!

Decomposers — Key Role

Fungi, bacteria, and earthworms break down dead matter → release nutrients back to soil → trees absorb them → cycle continues.

Chipko Movement

1973, Uttarakhand. Villagers hugged trees to stop cutting. "Chipko" = to hug. Symbol of grassroots conservation in India.

Forest Rights Act, 2006

Legally recognises tribal communities as rightful inhabitants and protectors of India's forests.

India's Forest Cover (2021)

7,13,789 sq km total. Only 3% is "very dense." 44.6% is open/degraded forest — under severe threat.

Key Terms
  • EcosystemAll living and non-living things in an area interacting as a system
  • DecomposersOrganisms (fungi, bacteria) that break down dead matter, recycling nutrients back to soil
  • TranspirationProcess by which plants release water vapour through leaves, contributing to the water cycle
  • DeforestationLarge-scale clearing of forests for agriculture, urban use, or industry
  • BiodiversityThe variety of all living species in a given area or on Earth
  • Carbon SinkA natural system (like a forest) that absorbs more CO₂ than it releases, slowing climate change
  • CanopyThe dense upper layer of branches and leaves formed by tall trees in a forest
